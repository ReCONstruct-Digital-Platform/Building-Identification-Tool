Q{{form.field_ordering.self_similar_cluster}}: Self-Similar Clusters

Definition:

A building is part of a self-similar cluster if it close to other buildings built at the same time, following the same construction methods, and using the same materials. Most of the time, they will seem virtually identical copies.

Relevance:

This is important for the value case of retrofits, as a multi-building project has economies of scale which makes retrofitting a cluster more attractive than retrofitting a single building.

Note:

Presence of the Multiple Addresses tag makes this more likely as the evaluation unit could contain multiple buildings.

Q{{form.field_ordering.has_simple_footprint}}: Building Footprint

Definition:

A building has a simple footprint if its intersection with the ground is through one or two simple geometric shapes (one or two rectangles). A complex footprint can have many corners, irregular angles, or multiple shapes of different types.

Note:

This is usually more readily visible from the satellite view of the building.

Q{{form.field_ordering.has_simple_volume}}: Volumetric Shape

Definition:

A simple volumetric form is a rectangular volume or a combination of a few rectangular volumes. A complex volumetric form can be any irregular volumes, often consisting of free-flowing curves, or of many rectangular volumes creating many angles.

Q{{form.field_ordering.num_storeys}}: Number of Storeys

Definition:

Any storey (floor) above the surface of the ground is a storey above-ground. The basement is not considered a storey.

Q{{form.field_ordering.has_basement}}: Basement

Definition:

Any floor partly or entirely submerged in the ground is a basement. A building is likely to have a basement if there are windows between the ground level and the first floor.

Q{{form.field_ordering.site_obstructions}}: Site Obstructions

Definition:

Please select any potential barriers that would hinder a crane's access to the building façade, such as: power lines or very large/old trees within 3m of the building, or another building less than 3m away.

Relevance:

Common retrofit approaches include installing panel solutions by crane, therefore obstructions may create challenges in the installation process and even make a panelized retrofit infeasible.

Q{{form.field_ordering.appendages}}: Appendages

Definition:

Appendages are any noticeable extruding features on the building including: balconies, large roof overhangs, or decorative protrusions.

Relevance:

Appendages increase the complexity of doing panelized retrofits, as they will have to be removed in most cases. Regarding balconies, they are often a major cause of thermal bridging which leads to heat loss and energy inefficiency.

Note:

Considering most people are attached to their balconies and they have to be removed for panelized retrofits, they could be a source of conflict and work against the project starting. Balconies can always be added back over the panels, but at a higher project cost.

Q{{form.field_ordering.facade_condition}}: Façade Condition

Definition:

A façade in poor condition may include: cladding material in poor shape with multiple areas displaying mold infestation and/or rot, noticeable water stains that can be identified at first glance, or if the overall condition of the exterior façade appears to be due for renovation.

Relevance:

Buildings with façades in poor condition are already in need of renovation at which point a panelized retrofit can be done at little extra cost, and provide additional benefits. We call these "anyway" retrofits.

Q{{form.field_ordering.large_irregular_windows}}: Large or Irregular Windows

Definition:

A window is considered very large when it is larger than 2m (the usual height of a door) in any direction.

A window is considered irregularly shaped if it is not a rectangle.

Relevance:

Very large windows contribute significantly to heating and cooling loss, therefore buildings with large windows are likely to be energy inefficient, and therefore good candidates for retrofits.

Irregularly shaped windows make a prefabricated panelized retrofit more difficult as panels need to be cut to shape. Additionally, they make the replication and scaling up of solutions infeasible since panels must be addapted to the specific window.

Q{{form.field_ordering.roof_geometry}}: New or Renovated Buildings

Definition:

If the building was built after the year 2000 or visually appears to be in exceptionally good condition with evidence of recent renovation, we can assume it has been recently renovated.

Relevance:

New and renovated buildings are very poor candidates for retrofits since they are already in good condition and are likely to have decent energy performance. Retrofitting these buildings is most likely unjustifiable from a business/value case perspective, considering building investments are made with a 30-40y horizon.
